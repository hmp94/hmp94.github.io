Honours Research Intern 2025
University of Amsterdam · Dr. Filip van Opstal
Studied mental resource depletion via pupillometry, testing whether resources depleted by one cognitive domain can interfere with another.
Pupillometry Cognitive Resources Mind Wandering
Details

It has been known that when one gets tired they tend to lose focus and mind-wander. The scientific way to frame this: mental resource depletion causes mind-wandering. However, it is unknown whether depleting mental resources by one module (e.g., spatial processing during a mental rotation task) causes mind-wandering in a different module (e.g., verbal processing).

This study investigates this by first depleting mental resources with one task in one period, then either switching or continuing the task in subsequent periods. We aim to see whether switching vs. continuing the same/different task causes any difference in mind-wandering, operationalized by the frequency and distance of pupil position away from the task-centered location.

Procedure
Period 1: ART (spatial · baseline)
Indicate whether the rotated letter is mirrored or normal
↓ Resources Depletion Phase ↓
Period 2: Switch or Continue
Dot Rotation (spatial · continue)
Indicate whether the relative position of 3 dots changed after rotation
Sternberg (verbal · switch)
Indicate whether the lowercase probe appeared in the memorized sequence
↓
Period 3: ART (spatial · post-depletion)
Same task as Period 1 — measure mind-wandering change
Main DV: Difference in mind-wandering (pupil deviation from task center) between Period 3 and Period 1. If switching modules restores resources, the switch condition should show less mind-wandering increase than the continue condition.

Personal Project
Bachelor's Thesis 2025 — 2026
University of Amsterdam · Dr. Romke Rouw
Investigated cross-dimensional tactile interference between perceived softness and stickiness. Methods included logistic regression and Python-based data analysis and visualization.
Tactile Perception Psychophysics
Details

Whenever we touch a surface, we immediately map its quality onto some subjective tactile description such as how soft it is and how sticky it is. However, this map is not one-to-one, such that we can experience the same tactile feeling with different materials. This study explores under which interaction between physical properties people can perceive the same/different tactile level, in this case of softness and stickiness.

The result of my Bachelor thesis reveals that people are more likely to perceive two stimuli as having different perceptual stickiness whether or not they are physically different in stickiness. Interestingly, higher physical softness difference is more likely to trick people this way. Furthermore, the relationship does not reverse, such that physical stickiness does not affect perceptual softness. My personal interpretation of this is thinking of the relative time difference you perceive these two. Softness is perceived when you press onto the surface, and stickiness is when you move away from it. Therefore you can only perceive stickiness after softness, but not the vice versa.
